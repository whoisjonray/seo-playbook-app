Full Strategy Details

Proper Redirect Use for SEO Preservation

What It Is: Using 301 redirects instead of 302 redirects during domain or URL migrations to permanently transfer SEO equity such as backlinks and rankings.
Why It Works: A 301 redirect signals to search engines that a page has permanently moved, allowing the full link equity and ranking signals to pass to the new URL, maintaining organic traffic and search visibility. In contrast, 302 redirects are temporary and do not transfer link value, leading to loss of rankings.
When To Use: During site migrations, domain changes, URL restructuring, or rebranding efforts where SEO authority must be preserved.
Execution Steps:

  • Before migration, map all old URLs to their new counterparts.
  • Implement 301 redirects on the server level (e.g., .htaccess, nginx config) or via CMS plugins for each URL.
  • Test redirects with tools like Screaming Frog or redirect checkers to ensure 301 status codes are properly set.
  • Update internal links and sitemap to reflect new URLs.
  • Monitor traffic and rankings closely post-migration for issues.
    Pitfalls & Limits:
  • Using 302 (temporary) redirects unintentionally results in lost link equity.
  • Redirect chains (multiple redirects in a row) dilute equity and slow crawling.
  • Not updating all URLs properly can cause loss of traffic.
  • Redirecting irrelevant pages can confuse users and search engines.
    Example: Threads migrated from threads.net to threads.com using 301 redirects, preserving backlinks and allowing SEO growth, whereas X (Twitter) used 302 redirects during their migration and lost 17 years of link equity, causing a drop in clicks and keyword rankings.
